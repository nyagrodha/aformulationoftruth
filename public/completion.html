You are not one self.

This is not a tragedy. It is more like weather.

I knew a man once who answered the Proust Questionnaire. It was 1987. His idea of perfect happiness? "A cabin in Montana with good light for reading."

In 1997 happenstantially coming upon the questionnaire that had shifted from within a box and now stained with his soon-to-be crusty sfacim, again he answered. His idea of perfect happiness? Well that had become "for my daughter to stop crying at night."

In 2007 it was "to finish the book I'm writing."

In 2017 it was, like clockwork, "a cabin in Montana with good light for reading."

He thought this meant something. Therein lies the ego's rub not upon the phallusper se. And it did. It meant something the way weather means something.

Lacan was a French psychoanalyst. From today's standard he smoked too many cigarettes. He said things like "the very foundation of interhuman dialogue is misunderstanding.", "the unconscious is structured like a language."; "I think where I am not; therefore, I am where I do not think."

He also said the self, this thing we call 'I' in English is basically a grammatical error. Imagine that bookish cunt we all know start correcting people for saying 'I'... apologies in advance.

We all call ourselves by the same name: I. It's who we are and yet when we say I, per Lacanian theory, we aren't referring to ourselves. Rather, I appeals to a phoneme‚Äîthis word I‚Äîto stand in where oneself ought be. But instead there is this grammatical anomaly.

PƒÅ·πáini was an ancient Sanskrit grammarian. He wrote a grammar so precise it could compile computer code if computers had existed in 400 BCE. His rules for how language works are like instructions for a very small machine that builds sentences one atom at a time.

Here's what PƒÅ·πáini did that matters: he never gave you a cozy definition like "the nominative case marks the subject." He did something sharper. The nominative‚Äîthe case of I, the case of naming‚Äîonly appears when nothing else has claimed the noun. It's a case of exclusion. Of what remains.

In Sanskrit grammar, you don't get to be "I" because you're the doer or the thinker. You get to be "I" because no other relationship seized you first. The nominative is the case of residue. What's left over when every other connection has been mapped and named.

Think about that. "I" is what remains when nothing else applies. Like the empty space in the pot. Like consciousness recognizing itself in a play. The subject isn't a thing. It's an absence that appears when all the relationships have been accounted for and there's still... this. Whatever this is.

Lacan would have loved this. The grammatical anomaly isn't a bug. It's the feature. We're a remainder. A leftover. The space that appears when you've mapped everything else.

This is why talking about yourself feels like trying to catch a trout with your hands.

The questionnaire has thirty-five questions.

"What is your greatest fear?"
"What is the trait you most deplore in yourself?"
"How would you like to die?"

These are not polite questions. They are holes in the rapidly melting ice.

If you answer honestly, spontaneously, something cold touches your feet.

If we think about this too much, we're likely students of Christine Korsgaard's, a Kantian ethicist (retired) at Harvard.

Anyhow 'stine says the self is not something we find. It's something we make. And from birth until death we're making a quilt, our lives. Quilts on quilts on quilts--every irl choice a kind of sewing.

We stitch ourselves together (or not!) out of what's available: mother's phrases, a teacher's posture, songs we heard when we were angsty teens, that stranger's coat I saw once at Cafe Roma and never forgot (it was nice!).

We are a quilt. We are only ever made of other people's fabrics. These days in the hellscape that is later-ish (?) capitalizm, priority is given to individuals, and we make like brands to sell a vanishingly fleeting formulation. A you de parvence variously seeking fortune, fame, and all things incompatibile with the realities of being a quilt. A family.

This is not sad. Quilts are warm. This has been brought to you by extending the metaphor.

The instruction is this: answer once. Then do your best to forget the questions. That dude up there he answered it four times. Frankly, tha's a lot.

This is important.

If we think about the questions too often, our responses start to become performative. It creates this vicious cycle that, once embarked upon wittingly or not, the ego's in control and the value of the mechanic is diminished. We soon (so soon!) start to believe the answers to be who we are. But questions are not who you, or I, or anyone else are. They are who we were on a Tuesday in November when our I's were tired and the light was grey.

Ten years later, having all but perfectly forgotten, we'll be someone else. That person, who is sitting in room different than the one you are in now, may answer the same questions and experience a good dose of that camatkƒÅra.

The questions don't change. I do.

This is like a river passing the same bridge twice. The bridge thinks it's seeing the same river. The river knows better.

Lacan thought up a concept he called objet petit a. The little object. The thing we're always looking for but can never find.

It's the hole in your sould that makes you open the refrigerator late at night/early in the AM when you're not even hungry.

It's the hole in your ass... I mean, self-understanding that makes anyone think to answer a questionnaire in the first place!

We're seeking something. We don't know what that is. Engaged thusly a seeker, we wouldn't recognize it. But the looking the perfecting feels important.

The looking is the thing.

In 1890, when Proust answered these questions somewhere in Paris, a person might be one thing for their whole life. A baker. A countess. A disappointment to their father.

If we are not one self, I don't have to defend any particular self, any version of me against the others. I do get to watch though as come for the second time in a day and go like clouds.

Clouds are beautiful. Nobody argues with clouds.

What is your idea of perfect happiness?

The question is a door.

Behind the door is another door.

Behind that door is a room with no floor, only a cloud-filled sky.

You've been falling through that sky your whole life.

How did the clouds even get inside? And where am I falling to?

The fall is the happiness.

Whatever nature may there be is in the fall.

Answer. Wait ten years. Answer again.

Notice "different" and "same" are words, and we are not a word.

We are the ones using the words with one another.

We are the ones who put down or lift up. And all of us do both. But we gotta practice doing more the lifting up one another.
