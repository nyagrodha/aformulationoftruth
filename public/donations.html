The Poet's Mind

ಕವಿಯ ಮನ • kaviya mana

by Kuvempu (ಕುವೆಂಪು)

ಕನ್ನಡ ಮೂಲ • Kannada Original

ಕವಿಯ ಮನ ನಂದನವನ;
ಆನಂದದ ರಸನಿಕೇತನ:
ಅಲ್ಲಿ ಚಾಡಿವಾ ಪ್ರಥಮ ಮಾಹಿಮ;
ಅಲ್ಲಿ ಯೋಭವಾ ಸುಖದ ಸಾವನ;
ಅಲ್ಲಿ ಸಾವಾ ಅಮೃತಯೋಗನ!
— ಕುವೆಂಪು

Transliteration

kaviya mana nandanavana;
ānandada rasanikētana:
alli cāḍivā prathama māhima;
alli yōbhavā sukhada sāvana;
alli sāvā amṛtayōgana!

English Translation

The poet's mind is a garden of delight;
The dwelling place of the essence of bliss:
There blooms the first majesty;
There arises the death of happiness;
There death becomes union with immortality!

Commentary

Kuvempu (1904-1994), one of the greatest Kannada poets and the first recipient of the Jnanpith Award for Kannada literature, presents here a profound meditation on the nature of the poet's consciousness.

The poem operates through a striking dialectical movement. The poet's mind begins as a nandanavana (नन्दनवन), a garden of delight—the same word used for Indra's celestial garden in Sanskrit literature. It is the rasanikētana (रसनिकेतन), the dwelling place where rasa (aesthetic essence) and ānanda (bliss) reside.

But Kuvempu's vision moves beyond mere celebration. In this garden, prathama māhima (the first or primal majesty) blooms—perhaps the original creative impulse, the urge to create that precedes all poetry. Yet immediately following this flowering comes a paradox: sukhada sāvana—the death of happiness itself.

This is the crucial turn in the poem. The poet's consciousness, which began as pure delight, must undergo a death—not of the self, but of mere happiness, of ordinary pleasure. This death is necessary for transformation. For in that very death (sāva), there occurs amṛtayōgana—union with the immortal, the deathless, the nectar of immortality.

Kuvempu thus articulates a vision familiar to mystics across traditions: that true transcendence requires a dying to the merely pleasant, a sacrifice of happiness for something greater—the union with the eternal that lies beyond both happiness and sorrow. The poet's mind is the site where this sacred death and resurrection continuously unfolds.

The poem's structure mirrors its content: five lines that move from garden to death to deathlessness, each line building on the last with the repeated alli (there)—emphasizing that all these movements occur within the single space of the poet's consciousness. The exclamation mark at the end signals both wonder and affirmation: this paradoxical truth about the poet's mind is not a tragedy but a cause for celebration.

About Kuvempu

Kuvempu (1904-1994), born Kuppali Venkatappa Puttappa, was a towering figure in Kannada literature and Indian philosophy. He served as the Vice-Chancellor of Mysore University and was the first Kannada writer to receive the Jnanpith Award, India's highest literary honor.

His philosophy of Viśva Mānavātavāda (Universal Humanism) emphasized the unity of all humanity while celebrating cultural diversity. His epic poem Śrī Rāmāyaṇa Darśanam reinterprets the Ramayana through a humanistic lens, and his novel Malegalalli Madumagalu explores the clash between tradition and modernity.

Kuvempu's poetry combines the classical Sanskrit poetic tradition with modern sensibilities, drawing from both Vedantic philosophy and contemporary concerns. His work reflects a deep engagement with nature, spirituality, and the human condition—themes that resonate powerfully in the post-independence Indian context.
